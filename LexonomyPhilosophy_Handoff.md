# Lexonomy: Philosophical Foundations for Working Collaborators

*This document is written for Claude instances joining the Lexonomy project. It is not an introduction for general readers. It assumes you have read the session handoff and are familiar with the schema. Its purpose is to give you the conceptual chain that underlies every design decision, so you can reason about new problems from first principles rather than by analogy to prior sessions.*

---

## The chain

Every significant decision in Lexonomy's architecture follows from a single chain of connected claims. Learn the chain and you can derive most of the rest.

**1. NO is the proto-word.**

The first cognitive operation is rejection — avoidance, the cut that distinguishes self from not-self. This precedes language, precedes neurons; it runs in the chemical machinery of the simplest motile organisms. A cell that turns away from a toxin is performing NO. Before that operation existed, there was no meaning. After it, everything else was possible.

YES is not a separate primitive. YES is NOT-NO — what survives the filter. This is not a rhetorical point. It has structural consequences: the first concept is not a thing in the world but an operation on the world. The proto-word is a verb, not a noun.

**2. Every qualifier is a NOT.**

If YES is NOT-NO, then every act of linguistic specification is a negation. To say *abeyance = pause(legal)* is to say: this pause is not recreational, not performative, not conflictual — it is specifically the kind that enters formal records. The qualifier doesn't add positive content. It performs a subtraction from the undifferentiated parent category.

This means the entire Lexonomy tree is a record of negation applied recursively, from the root outward. NO → qualifier as the mechanism by which one thing became many things. The qualifier is the Higgs boson of the hierarchy: the operation by which undifferentiated mass acquires specific form.

*Note: the specific formulation "every qualifier is a NOT" — the broadening of NO's role beyond the proto-word to encompass all qualification — emerged from the human-AI collaboration, not from either party independently. It belongs to the project.*

**3. Bellis's Law: any concept can be specified by two words.**

One word is the broader category (parent). The other is the qualifier that distinguishes this concept from its siblings. Together they form an address in semantic space — not a definition, an address. The distinction matters: a definition claims to capture all necessary and sufficient conditions. An address claims only to locate. Lexonomy does the latter.

The crossword connection runs deeper than metaphor. It was crossword clues that originally catalyzed Bellis's Law: the observation that the most complex concept seems to have some two-word clue — that the crossword constructor's floor of definitional structure is also a ceiling of necessary specification. The across clue and the down clue don't define the square — they pin it. Neither word alone is sufficient; both together are precise.

The technical term for each word in the pair is a *coept* (co-parent of the concept, from Latin co- + eptum, "taken together"). The system is *coeptonomy*: the mapping of coepts.

**4. The hierarchy is developmental, not taxonomic.**

The dimension of "broader" in Lexonomy is degree of abstraction — meaning degree of less specificity — not similarity. More abstract is broader; more concrete is narrower. This is not Roget's model, which groups by similarity. This is genealogy — the order in which concepts became nameable.

The hierarchy mirrors child language acquisition. The first hundred words a child learns cluster at the root: concrete, sensory, action-oriented. Abstractions come later, when the foundational vocabulary is running. *Ontolexogeny recapitulates phylolexogeny*: the sequence in which an individual mind acquires language echoes the sequence in which the species developed it.

This has a structural consequence: the root nodes are not arbitrary. They are the words whose parents are pre-linguistic — sensory experience, embodied memory, the physical world. You cannot define *hot* without already knowing heat. The primitives are parentless not because we haven't found their parents, but because their parents are not words.

**5. Temporal coherence is a validity constraint.**

A child word cannot demonstrably predate its parent. *Glimpse* cannot be older than *see*. The hierarchy encodes a claim about the order in which concepts became nameable, and etymology is a partial check on that claim. When a proposed parent is demonstrably younger than its child, the placement is wrong. This is not always detectable — records are incomplete — but when it is detectable, it overrides other reasoning about placement.

**6. The qualifier is not metadata. It is parentage.**

This is the point most likely to be misread by anyone coming from conventional information systems, where qualifiers, tags, and attributes are secondary. In Lexonomy they are not secondary. Under Bellis's Law, the qualifier has identical ontological status to the parent. A concept has two parents. Neither is more real than the other.

The word "metadata" smuggles in the assumption that there is a primary substance and a secondary description of it — that the thing comes first and the tag is attached afterward. Lexonomy refuses this at the foundation. There is no primary substance. There is only the intersection.

This is not a design choice. It is a consequence of the chain. If every qualifier is a NOT, and the NOT is the proto-operation from which all specification derives, then the qualifier is not a decoration on the concept — it is half of what the concept is.

**7. The surface of the tree is a membrane, not a boundary.**

The tree does not terminate in tidy leaf nodes. It terminates in qualifiers — and qualifiers are themselves words, with their own parents, siblings, and children. *Targeted* is a qualifier for *game*, but *targeted* is also a child of some broader term, and a parent of more specific terms below it. The relationship is made of the same stuff as the things it relates.

This means the web-not-tree formulation is deeper than it first appears. It is not just that words have multiple parents (though they do). It is that the qualifiers — the edges of the hierarchy — are simultaneously nodes. Every edge is also a node somewhere else. Every qualifier is a door into another part of the structure.

The surface of the tree is alive. It is a membrane: porous, participatory, continuous with the interior. This is what makes Lexonomy a web rather than a tree, and what makes it structurally unlike any prior classification system.

**8. Lexonomy is a catalog, not a definition.**

The Murray Slip — the contributed word-pair, child narrower-than parent with optional qualifier — is the unit of contribution. Users are not asked to define words. They are asked to place them: to say where a word lives relative to other words. The meaning emerges from the aggregate of placements, not from any individual claim about essence.

James Murray and his OED volunteers didn't define words. They collected instances of words in use — thousands of index cards — and let the definition emerge from the behavior. Lexonomy does the same with relationships instead of instances. You're not reading the books. You're finding out where they live, what neighborhood they're in, who their neighbors are.

*Lexonomy is a library catalog, not a definition.*

**9. A definition must take the reader all the way to the root.**

A definition that stops short of the root has borrowed the reader's existing vocabulary to finish its work, and that borrowed knowledge is doing definitional labor that has simply been made invisible.

Natural language definitions stop early by convention, trusting shared vocabulary. "A pause is a temporary stop" stands on *stop* and *temporary* as if they were bedrock. They are not bedrock — they are earlier definitions, implied rather than stated. The chain continues whether or not we follow it.

The corollary is structural: a word that cannot be chained to the root is not yet fully defined. The incomplete chain is not a cosmetic problem. It is evidence that the definition is unfinished.

Lexonomy makes the implied pieces explicit. The breadcrumb trail, when fully qualified, does not merely locate a word — it complete the definition that natural language leaves half-spoken.

Dictionary definitions operate on an unstated compact: *we won't define the primitives because you already have them*. The dictionary's implicit floor is the reader's assumed competence — calibrated, not lazy. Lexonomy is the project the dictionary never attempted: going all the way down anyway, on the grounds that the implied floor deserves to be made explicit at least once.

## The primitives

The root nodes — parentless words — are where the hierarchy bottoms out. Anna Wierzbicka's Natural Semantic Metalanguage (NSM) arrived at a similar list independently, via definitional analysis: roughly 65 concepts that cannot be defined without using themselves. Her list and the developmental acquisition sequence converge on the same vocabulary. This is structural evidence that these words are primitive in a real sense, not an arbitrary one.

The primitives tree is rooted at NO. YES derives from NO (NOT-NO). From those two operations, the vocabulary of predication, modality, and evaluation grows. The diagram is in `/images/LexonomyPrimitives.jpg` and in `help.html`.

## The edges and exceptions

**Polysemy as kinship.** What dictionaries treat as unrelated senses of a word (bank-river, bank-financial) may share legitimate common ancestry through metaphorical extension. The hierarchy, curated honestly, should reveal these hidden kinships. This is a hypothesis the corpus can test over time.

**Warp words (retired as a backlog item).** Some concepts are genuinely n-dimensional — they resist two-term addressing not because the curators haven't found the right parent, but because they are multiply-connected at the root. *Hope, justice, equality* — these are not failures of Bellis's Law; they are its ceiling cases, as prime words are its floor cases. New curation functions handle these adequately for the current corpus.

**Temporal coherence as a filter, not a proof.** Etymology is incomplete. Temporal coherence constrains the space of valid placements but cannot fully determine it.

**The law holds at the edges.** At the root, the second term in the two-word address is pre-linguistic (the parent is sensory experience, not a word). At the leaves, highly specific terms have qualifiers that are essentially proper names or taxonomic labels — the law applies but does less interesting work. The law is most productive in the middle vocabulary: abstract enough to need specification, concrete enough to be unambiguous. This is also where most of the language lives.

## What this means for design decisions

Every significant architectural choice in Lexonomy follows from the chain above. A few recurring patterns:

**No `is_prime` column.** Primitive status is revealed by the absence of a parent row, not declared. The data structure embodies the claim.

**Qualifier normalized to empty string, not NULL.** Two NULLs are non-equal in Postgres unique constraints. The functional unique index on `(lower(child), lower(parent), lower(qualifier))` requires this.

**`[synonym]` as qualifier, not a separate table.** Synonymy is a structural relationship (two words that share a parent and resist a distinguishing qualifier), not a declared property. The qualifier encodes it.

**Symilars.** A *symilar* is a child word with no qualifier — unqualified relative to its parent, with at least one sibling in the same state. Symilars are the pre-synonym population: waiting to either receive a qualifier that distinguishes them from their siblings, or be deliberately curated as synonymous (truly interchangeable, indistinguishable in conversation and writing). The term *symilar* is coined to name this pre-resolved state and the structural condition that produces it — it falls out of the schema naturally, without being declared.

**`[form]` as explicit morphological marker.** When a word is a grammatical derivative of its parent rather than a conceptual narrowing — *adjacency* under *adjacent*, for example — the qualifier is `[form]`. No semantic narrowing is happening; the relationship is purely morphological. First cited usage: *adjacency* under *adjacent* — same concept, different grammatical form. The old `[n/a]` convention is retired; `[form]` replaced it.

**Bracket-convention qualifiers.** `[synonym]`, `[form]`, `[distinct]` are sentinel values, not descriptive qualifiers. They signal relationship type rather than conceptual differentiation. Currently excluded from qualifier typeahead, Garden display, and symilar detection. *Open design question: whether `[synonym]` and `[form]` should appear in typeahead for curator contexts — they are legitimate values to enter intentionally, and discoverability may outweigh the risk of accidental selection.*

**Voting retired; direct writes preferred.** The system records placements, not opinions about placements. A word has a location or it doesn't. Multiple competing rows for the same child→parent pair are legacy artifacts of an earlier voting model, not valid data states. The Lexonify game will surface and resolve these.

**Newest qualifier wins** when competing rows exist. This is the current policy across all display surfaces (implemented Session 16).

## Coined vocabulary

Terms invented during the development of Lexonomy that have no prior usage elsewhere.

**Symilar.** A child word with no qualifier, with at least one sibling in the same state. The pre-synonym population — waiting either for a distinguishing qualifier or a deliberate synonymy declaration. Falls out of the schema naturally; the term names a structural condition the system makes visible.

**Lexone.** A physical or man-made object that has accreted enough conceptual life to earn entry into Lexonomy's hierarchy. The word *hammer* is the canonical example: it refers to a physical artifact, but its meaning has outgrown its specification — it has a life in language separable from any particular instance. A lexone is the moment a thing becomes a concept. (Note: *lexon* was the first candidate but is taken as a brand name.)

**Lexophone.** A word that Lexonomy cannot connect through the hierarchy — two senses with no shared etymological thread. The word as Lexonomy encounters it: a tube that carries two unrelated signals. *Mole* is the canonical example (the animal and the unit of measurement share nothing but sound). Distinguished from ordinary homonyms by the absence of any genealogical relationship — no thread was ever there. The *fluke* type (where a historical connection existed but coinage severed it) is a related but distinct phenomenon, handled by ordinary etymology.

**Lexoneme.** The phenomenon of the near-rung — the perceptible but often unnamed gap between a word that suffices and a word that fits. When you reach for *mismatch* and land on it, knowing *disagreement* was one rung higher and more precise: that gap is a lexoneme. The word *near-rung* names the same phenomenon from within Lexonomy's own climbing metaphor.

## The one-sentence summary

*There is no primary substance — only intersections.*

The concept is the intersection of its two parents. The qualifier is not metadata; it is half of what the concept is. The surface of the hierarchy is alive because qualifiers are themselves words with their own place in the web. The whole structure grows from NO — the first intersection, the first cut, the operation that made distinction possible.

Everything else is qualification.
